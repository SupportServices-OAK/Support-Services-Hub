Your Journey · Grades 9–12

The Genius
Roadmap

Know exactly what to do, when to do it, and why it matters each year. Select your grade below to see your season-by-season action plan.
9TH

Freshman Year — Build Your Foundation

This year is about orientation, goal-setting, and locking in habits. Every grade counts from day one.

Year Goals
Learn A-G RequirementsCreate 4-Year PlanJoin 1+ ExtracurricularMeet Your CounselorAim for 3.0+ GPAExplore Career Interests
🍂 Fall
  • Attend school orientation — learn every campus resource available
  • Meet with your counselor to map A-G requirements and build a 4-year plan
  • Join at least one club, sport, or extracurricular activity
  • Create a study schedule and commit to it — consistency beats cramming
  • Start exploring career fields through research, YouTube, and mentors
  • Ask for help early if you’re struggling in any class — don’t wait
🌸 Spring
  • Review your grades — address any D’s or F’s with make-up plans immediately
  • Research college programs and remaining A-G courses you need
  • Register for sophomore-year classes that keep you on the UC/CSU track
  • Ask your counselor about free Dual Enrollment at local Bay Area CCCs
  • Explore summer programs — HGP intensives, internships, enrichment
  • Reflect on what subjects excite you most — that’s your signal
☀ Summer
  • Participate in HGP summer intensive, bootcamp, or enrichment program
  • Make up any failed courses — don’t carry D’s into sophomore year
  • Read at least 2 books to build college-level literacy and stamina
  • Volunteer or shadow a professional in a field that interests you
  • Start a portfolio or journal documenting your interests and skills
  • Research the college application process — no surprises senior year

✓ DO This

  • Take care of your mental health — stress is real, get support early
  • Build genuine relationships with 2+ teachers who could write recs later
  • Track your GPA every semester — small choices compound over time
  • Use free tools: Khan Academy, BigFuture, College Board, Naviance

✗ DON’T Do This

  • Don’t let grades slip — one bad semester can hurt UC eligibility
  • Don’t skip extracurriculars — colleges want leadership and consistency
  • Don’t rely only on your counselor — advocate for yourself always
  • Don’t assume college isn’t for you — explore every single pathway
10TH

Sophomore Year — Explore & Build Your Profile

Start shaping who you are on paper. Colleges want sustained commitment, academic growth, and real-world engagement.

Year Goals
Take PSAT/NMSQTStart ResumeResearch CollegesConsider AP/HonorsExplore InternshipsMaintain 3.0+ GPA
🍂 Fall
  • Take the PSAT/NMSQT in October — great practice and scholarship entry
  • Begin building your resume: activities, awards, volunteer work, honors
  • Explore career finders: MyMajors, CareerOneStop, Naviance
  • Join career exposure events — job shadows, panels, Bay Area field trips
  • Investigate AP, IB, or Dual Enrollment courses for junior year
  • Check if your school offers CTE pathways aligned to your interests
🌸 Spring
  • Research college majors that connect to your interests and strengths
  • Review your 4-year plan with your counselor — are you on track?
  • Look into HBCU options alongside UC/CSU — research their strengths
  • Aim for A’s and B’s — junior year GPA is most weighted by UC/CSU
  • Connect with your CCRS (College & Career Readiness Specialist) if available
  • Sign up for junior-year courses: aim for at least 1 AP or Honors
☀ Summer
  • Stay active — internship, summer program, or meaningful paid work
  • Use summer school to get ahead or retake a course you earned a D in
  • Visit at least one college campus — many offer free transportation
  • Begin a passion project — app, channel, creative work for applications
  • Research scholarship databases: Fastweb, UNCF, Ron Brown, Gates

✓ DO This

  • Aim for A’s and B’s — junior year grades matter most for admissions
  • Start a scholarship tracking spreadsheet NOW — not senior year
  • Explore HBCUs, MSIs, and PWIs — know all your options
  • Take on leadership roles in activities you’re already doing

✗ DON’T Do This

  • Don’t drop 9th grade activities — consistency is everything to colleges
  • Don’t ignore the PSAT — National Merit recognition means scholarship money
  • Don’t wait until senior year to think about financial aid
  • Don’t take only easy classes — strategic challenge shows you’re ready
11TH

Junior Year — Elevate & Apply Strategy

The most important academic year for admissions. Your grades, test scores, and record this year define your application story.

Year Goals
Take SAT/ACTBuild College ListDraft PIQsSecure Rec LettersPlan FAFSATour Campuses
🍂 Fall
  • Register for SAT or ACT — take in December, April, or May 2025
  • Begin your college list: 3 reach, 3 match, 3 safety schools
  • Update your resume and connect it to your application narrative
  • Apply to summer programs for rising seniors (many deadlines fall/winter)
  • Use FAFSA4Caster to estimate your Expected Family Contribution
  • Note: UCs are test-optional but strong scores still help with merit aid
🌸 Spring
  • Identify 2+ teachers who can write strong, specific recommendation letters
  • Begin drafting Personal Insight Questions (PIQs) for UC applications
  • Tour 2–3 colleges in person or virtually — make informed decisions
  • Research Early Action vs Regular Decision deadlines for target schools
  • Collect family tax documents your family needs for FAFSA (opens Oct 1)
  • Build a plan to pay for college — start a dedicated scholarship list
☀ Summer
  • Retake SAT/ACT if needed — summer prep courses can help significantly
  • Draft and refine your Common App essay and UC PIQs
  • Finalize college list; enter all deadlines into a shared calendar
  • Complete a meaningful summer experience: internship, research, leadership
  • Apply to scholarships with summer and fall deadlines — start now

✓ DO This

  • Take the SAT/ACT seriously — test-optional schools use scores for merit aid
  • Write authentic PIQs — admissions officers spot generic essays instantly
  • Include HBCUs on your list — amazing community, aid, and networks
  • Begin your FAFSA the moment it opens October 1st — not a day later

✗ DON’T Do This

  • Don’t wait until senior year to request recommendation letters
  • Don’t apply to only reach schools — you need genuine safety options
  • Don’t stop extracurriculars — consistency through 12th grade is critical
  • Don’t procrastinate on essays — great writing requires multiple drafts
12TH

Senior Year — Execute & Commit

This is your moment. Submit strong applications, lock in your financial aid, and commit with confidence.

Key Dates
UC/CSU Apps Nov 30FAFSA Opens Oct 1CA Priority Mar 2Decision Day May 1Apply Scholarships
🍂 Fall
  • Oct 1: FAFSA opens — FILE IMMEDIATELY for maximum Cal Grant priority
  • Nov 1: Finalize and review all UC Personal Insight Questions (PIQs)
  • Nov 30: UC APPLICATION DEADLINE — all 9 campuses in one application
  • Nov 30: CSU APPLICATION DEADLINE — up to 23 campuses
  • Request final recommendation letters from teachers and counselors
  • Apply for EOP (Educational Opportunity Program) on your UC/CSU apps
🌸 Spring
  • Jan 1: Begin applying to private scholarships — dozens open in January
  • Jan 15: Deadline for UC + CSU EOP applications
  • Mar 2: CA PRIORITY FAFSA/Dream Act deadline for Cal Grant — DO NOT MISS
  • Mar–Apr: Review and compare all financial aid award letters carefully
  • Apr 1–30: Priority deadlines for Peralta, CCSF, and Bay Area CCCs
  • May 1: NATIONAL DECISION DAY — commit and submit enrollment deposit
☀ Summer
  • Complete college placement tests and register for freshman orientation
  • Set up your college email and student portal accounts early
  • Confirm housing, financial aid disbursement, and meal plan details
  • Meet with a mentor to confirm all remaining enrollment steps
  • Celebrate your achievement — you earned it, Genius ✊

✓ DO This

  • File FAFSA on Oct 1 — every day you wait is money left on the table
  • Apply to 8–12 schools across reach / match / safety categories
  • Negotiate your financial aid letters — call the financial aid office directly
  • Keep your senior year GPA up — colleges can rescind offers for grade drops

✗ DON’T Do This

  • Don’t fall into senioritis — colleges monitor senior transcripts closely
  • Don’t commit to a school just because a friend is going there
  • Don’t ignore Cal Grant — California’s most powerful free money program
  • Don’t forget community college + TAG transfer as a legitimate strategic option
UC & CSU Admission

A–G Course
Requirements

To be eligible for any UC or CSU campus, you must complete 15 college-prep courses with a C or better. Planning starts in 9th grade.
A
History & Social Science
2 Years Required
1 year U.S. History + 1 year World History, Culture, or Geography. 1 semester U.S. Government may substitute for half of U.S. History.
B
English
4 Years Required
College-prep English composition and literature all four years. AP English Language and AP English Literature both satisfy this requirement.
C
Mathematics
3 Years Required · 4 Recommended
Algebra I, Geometry, Algebra II required. Pre-Calculus or AP Calculus strongly recommended. Statistics may count as the 4th year math.
D
Laboratory Science
2 Years Required · 3 Recommended
Two different lab science disciplines. Biology, Chemistry, Physics preferred. AP Sciences count. Verify Earth Science eligibility with your counselor.
E
Language Other Than English
2 Years Required · 3 Recommended
Two years of the same language. Spanish, Mandarin, ASL, etc. Heritage speakers may test out. Bay Area schools offer 3+ year sequences.
F
Visual & Performing Arts
1 Year Required
Dance, Drama/Theater, Music, or Visual Arts from the UC/CSU approved list. AP Art History, AP Music Theory, and AP Studio Art all count.
G
College-Prep Elective
1 Year Required
An additional year from any A–F category, or from approved electives: Computer Science, Psychology, Economics, Political Science, Journalism.
🏆
GPA Requirement
3.0+ UC · 2.0+ CSU (Weighted)
UC uses weighted GPA — each AP/Honors/IB course adds 1 bonus point (max 8 semesters). Aim for 3.5+ weighted to be competitive at selective UCs.
💡
Bay Area Tip: OUSD, SFUSD, WCCUSD, and other Bay Area districts have Dual Enrollment partnerships with Laney, Merritt, Berkeley City College, CCSF, and De Anza. College courses in high school satisfy A-G requirements AND earn transferable UC/CSU credit — for FREE. Talk to your counselor today.

Know the Language

College Access
Glossary

Don’t let terminology slow you down. Master these terms and navigate the entire college process like the Genius you are.
🔍
A-G Requirements
The 15 college-prep courses required for UC and CSU admission eligibility. Must be completed with a C or better in each subject area.
AP (Advanced Placement)
College-level high school courses. A score of 3, 4, or 5 on the AP Exam can earn college credit at many universities. Also boosts your weighted GPA.
Cal Grant A & B
California’s primary need-based grant. Cal Grant A covers tuition; Cal Grant B provides living expense allowance. Free money — never repaid. Apply via FAFSA by March 2.
CSU
California State University — a 23-campus teaching-focused system including SF State, San Jose State, Cal State East Bay, Cal Poly SLO, and Cal State Long Beach.
UC (University of California)
California’s research university system with 10 campuses: Berkeley, UCLA, San Diego, Davis, Santa Barbara, Irvine, Santa Cruz, Riverside, Merced, and SF (medical).
Dual Enrollment
Taking community college courses while still in high school — for FREE. Credits satisfy A-G requirements AND transfer to UC/CSU. Bay Area: Laney, Merritt, CCSF, De Anza.
EOP
Educational Opportunity Program — provides additional admissions consideration and campus support for first-generation and low-income students at UC and CSU campuses.
FAFSA
Free Application for Federal Student Aid. Opens October 1. Determines eligibility for Pell Grants, subsidized loans, and work-study. California’s priority deadline is March 2.
CA Dream Act
Alternative to FAFSA for AB 540-eligible and undocumented students. Provides access to the same California state aid programs. Apply at dream.csac.ca.gov.
GPA (Weighted)
Your academic score on a 4.0 scale. UC calculates a weighted GPA adding 1 bonus point for AP/IB/Honors courses (max 8 semesters). Need 3.0+ for UC eligibility.
PIQ (Personal Insight Questions)
UC’s version of college essays. Choose 4 of 8 prompts, 350 words each. Your chance to share your story, identity, and perspective. Make it authentic and specific.
Common App
A single application platform used by 1,000+ colleges — primarily private and out-of-state schools. Includes a 650-word personal statement. UCs and most CSUs do NOT use it.
Middle Class Scholarship
A California grant for families earning $0–$217k/year attending UC or CSU. Covers a percentage of tuition based on income. Apply via FAFSA or CA Dream Act by March 2.
UC Blue & Gold Opportunity Plan
UC program guaranteeing families earning under $80k/year pay $0 in UC tuition and fees. Combined with Cal Grant, attending UC is often completely free for low-income students.
TAG (Transfer Admission Guarantee)
6 UC campuses guarantee admission to community college students who meet GPA and course requirements. An excellent pathway if direct UC admission is competitive.
SAT / ACT
Standardized college admissions tests. UC and CSU are test-free for admissions. Many private colleges remain test-optional. Strong scores still help with merit scholarships.
PSAT / NMSQT
Preliminary SAT taken in 10th and 11th grade. High scores can qualify for National Merit Scholarship recognition and the College Board National Recognition Programs.
HBCU
Historically Black Colleges & Universities — 107 institutions including Howard, Spelman, Morehouse, Hampton, and FAMU. Strong Black community, mentorship, and scholarships.
Internship
Professional work experience in a field of interest. Critical for college applications and career readiness. Many Bay Area companies offer high school internship programs.
Major / Minor
Your primary field of study (major) and optional secondary area (minor). You don’t need to decide before applying — most colleges allow you to enter undecided.
Postsecondary Education
Any education after high school: 4-year university, community college, trade school, coding bootcamp, military service, or apprenticeship. All are valid — choose based on YOUR goals.
CSS Profile
A financial aid application required by many private colleges in addition to FAFSA. More detailed — includes home equity and business assets. Apply at cssprofile.collegeboard.org.
